# Humanoid Anatomy

A humanoid robot looks like a human — two arms, two legs, a head, and a torso. But inside, it is very different from a human body. Instead of bones and muscles, it has metal frames and motors. Instead of a nervous system, it has wires and computers.

## Body Structure

The body of a humanoid robot is built around a **skeleton** made of aluminum or carbon fiber. This frame is strong but light — just like our bones.

Connected to this frame are **joints** — the places where the robot can bend and rotate. A robot arm might have 7 joints (just like a human arm), allowing it to reach in any direction.

Think of a robot arm like a flexible drinking straw:
- Each bend point = one joint
- More joints = more flexibility = more things it can do

## Joints and Actuators

A **joint** is where movement happens. An **actuator** is the motor that creates that movement.

There are two main types of actuators:

**Electric motors** — the most common type. A small but powerful motor spins, and gears convert that spin into a precise movement. Most modern humanoids like Tesla Optimus use electric actuators.

**Hydraulic actuators** — use pressurized fluid to create very strong movements. Boston Dynamics' Atlas uses hydraulics, which is why it can jump and do backflips.

Each joint has a number called **degrees of freedom (DOF)**. One DOF means it can move in one direction (like a door hinge). More DOF means more complex movement.

A full humanoid robot typically has **30–50 degrees of freedom** across its whole body.

## Power and Control

Every robot needs power. Most humanoid robots use **lithium battery packs** — similar to what is in your phone but much larger. A typical humanoid can run for 1–2 hours on a single charge.

The **control computer** sits in the torso. It reads sensor data (cameras, touch sensors, gyroscopes) and sends commands to each motor dozens of times per second.

Think of it like a conductor in an orchestra: the computer is the conductor, and each motor is a musician. The conductor must coordinate all musicians perfectly to produce smooth, coordinated movement.

## Key Takeaway

A humanoid robot's body is carefully engineered to be strong, light, and flexible. Every joint, motor, and sensor serves a specific purpose — just like every part of the human body evolved for a reason. The more joints a robot has, the more human-like its movement can be.
